Why are priors useful
Denis Valle
March 2019

Learning objectives

People that are still learning Bayesian statistics hate priors. Priors are often seen as something that stands in our way (i.e., an obstacle) to get the information that we want. Common questions include:

  • How do we specify them anyway?
  • Better yet, how do we specify them in a way that does not interfere too much with the information I am going to extract from my data?

In reality, priors can actually help your analysis, if used well. The goal of this section is to illustrate how priors can be useful and why we do not always want uninformative priors.

Kidney cancer example

This section is based on an example on kidney cancer death rates, presented in (Gelman 1998). The figures below show the counties with the highest and lowest kidney cancer death rates. Is there any noticeable spatial pattern in these maps? If there is, why do we see these patterns?

How were these cancer death rates calculated?

Let \(y_i\) be the number of deaths and \(N_i\) be the population size for the i-th county. Because the probability of dying of kidney cancer \(\theta_i\) is typically very small while \(N_i\) is relatively large, a common model for these data is:

\[y_i\sim Poisson(\theta_i N_i)\] Because \(E[y_i ]=\theta_i N_i\), then an estimate of kidney cancer death risk is \(\hat{\theta}_i = y_i/N_i\). This is the estimate that is shown above in the maps.

What is the problem with these death rates?

The tricky thing here is that one cancer death in counties with very small populations can result in very high cancer death rate while no deaths in these counties result in very low death rates. In other words, counties with small populations are likely to either have a very high or very low \(\hat{\theta_i}=y_i/N_i\), which does not necessarily mean that cancer risk in this place is very high or very low.

Let’s generate some data to illustrate this phenomenon. Just for this example, we assume that \(\theta_i\) is a constant, equal to \(4.65\times 10^{-4}\) (the national level kidney cancer death risk). Then, we generate the number of deaths using the Poisson distribution above, using the known population sizes of FL counties. Do you see how the variation changes as a function of county population size?

set.seed(1)

setwd('U:/uf/courses/bayesian course/2016/chapter3c cancer')
pop=read.csv('FL county pop.csv',as.is=T)

N=nrow(pop)
theta=4.65*(10^(-4)) #this is our national level kidney cancer death risk
y=rpois(N,theta*pop$Population)

theta.freq=y/pop$Population 
plot(pop$Population,theta.freq,ylab='Estimate of kidney cancer death risk',xlab='Population size') #notice how variation is huge for counties with small populations
abline(h=theta,col='blue',lty=3)

What if kidney cancer death risk is not constant throughout the country?

What if kidney cancer death risk is not constant throughout the US and we are interested in estimating \(\theta_i\) for each county i? Is there a better estimate of cancer risk than \(\hat{\theta_i}=y_i/N_i\)?

Say we have prior information on the distribution of kidney cancer risks across the US (i.e., we know hyper-prior parameters a and b from the gamma distribution). Then, we can find the posterior distribution assuming a Poisson likelihood:

\[p(\theta_i|y_i) \propto p(y_i|\theta_i)p(\theta_i)\] \[p(\theta_i|y_i) \propto Poisson(y_i|\theta_i N_i)Gamma(\theta_i|a,b)\]

\[\propto (\theta_iN_i)^{y_i}exp(-\theta_i N_i)\theta_i^{a-1} exp(-\theta_i b)\] \[\propto \theta_i^{(y_i+a)-1} exp(-\theta_i(N_i+b))\] \[p(\theta_i|y_i)=Gamma(y_i+a,N_i+b)\] The mean of this posterior distribution is given by \(E(\theta_i|y_i)=\frac{y_i+a}{N_i+b}\).

Notice that, if we know nothing about the distribution of \(\theta_i\), then we could have set a=b=0 and obtained our earlier estimate. Indeed, many frequentists argue that we must not allow things external to the data at hand (e.g., prior knowledge) to influence our inference.

However, it might be beneficial to use the estimate \(\hat{\theta_i}=\frac{y_i+a}{N_i+b}\) because of the characteristics below:

  • if we do not have much information on a particular county (\(N_i\) and \(y_i\) are relatively small compared to a and b), then the prior will have a stronger effect and disease risk for that county will be more similar to the national level cancer risk.
  • if we have plenty of information (\(N_i\) and \(y_i\) are relatively big compared to a and b), then data dominate the prior. In other words, the exact prior parameters a and b do not matter all that much in estimating cancer risk for this county.

Which estimator should we use?

  1. Bayesian estimator \(\hat{\theta_i}=\frac{y_i+a}{N_i+b}\)
  2. Standard estimator \(\hat{\theta_i}=y_i/N_i\)

To answer this question, we will compare both methods. To do this, we will assume that:

\[p(\theta_i)=Gamma(10,\frac{10}{4.65\times 10^{-4}})\]

which has the desired mean \(E[\theta_i]=4.65\times 10^{-4}\) and \(p(\theta_i<0.001)=0.998\) (the probability that kidney cancer death risk is smaller than 0.1% is very high).

set.seed(2)

#get population data
setwd('U:/uf/courses/bayesian course/2015/chapter3c cancer')
pop=read.csv('FL county pop.csv',as.is=T)
# hist(pop$Population)

#generate fake data
N=nrow(pop)
a=10
b=10/(4.65*(10^(-4)))
theta=rgamma(N,a,b)
y=rpois(N,theta*pop$Population)

#calculate different estimators of kidney cancer death risk
theta.freq=y/pop$Population #frequentist estimator
theta.bayes=(y+a)/(pop$Population+b) #bayesian estimator

#make a quick figure showing true vs estimated
rango=range(c(theta,theta.freq,theta.bayes))
plot(theta,theta.freq,xlim=rango,ylim=rango,xlab='True death rate',ylab='Estimated death rate')
points(theta,theta.bayes,col='red')
lines(rango,rango)

#which estimator is best? 
#We are going to compare these estimators based on the sum-of-squared errors SSE
sum((theta-theta.freq)^2)
## [1] 7.683541e-07
sum((theta-theta.bayes)^2)
## [1] 4.343966e-07

These results suggest that the Bayesian estimator is more accurate than the standard estimator, suggesting that we are able to obtain better estimates of cancer death rate for each county by incorporating prior information. In other words, having the ability to use prior information is not necessarily bad, as often portrayed. One potential objection is that this was just luck… had we generated a different dataset, perhaps we would have obtained a different result. So let’s check this by creating multiple datasets and calculating the sum-of-squared-errors SSE for each of these dataset.

#we can do this multiple times to see which estimator is best
res=matrix(NA,1000,2)
for (i in 1:1000){
  theta=rgamma(N,a,b)
  y=rpois(N,theta*pop$Population)
  
  theta.freq=y/pop$Population 
  theta.bayes=(y+a)/(pop$Population+b)
  
  #which estimator is best?
  res.freq=sum((theta-theta.freq)^2)
  res.bayes=sum((theta-theta.bayes)^2)
  res[i,]=c(res.freq,res.bayes)
}
mean(res[,1]>res[,2]) #in almost all simulations (99.3%), theta.freq is worse than theta.bayes
## [1] 0.993

This code shows that, for almost all simulations, the standard estimator performed worse than the Bayesian estimator.

One might object to this example saying that it is not a fair comparison since we incorporated information on how the data were generated into our prior, which is very unrealistic in a real-world problem. There are two answers to this:

  1. in the real-world, we never know if we are right or not. However, if we had at least some information beyond that contained in our data, it should help us in the analysis. Unfortunately, there is no formal way of incorporating this information in a frequentist setting;

  2. even in the situation where we do not know the parameters a and b of the gamma distribution, we can estimate each \(\theta_i\) together with the prior parameters a and b! Doing so also typically leads to better performance than using \(\hat{\theta_i}=~y_i/N_i\) because of a phenomenon called shrinkage, which we will talk more about when dealing with mixed-effect models.

You can read more on this example in pages 55-62 (Gelman et al. 2004).
